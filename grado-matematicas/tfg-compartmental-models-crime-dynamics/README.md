# Compartmental Modeling of Crime Dynamics

## Overview

This repository contains my **Bachelor’s Thesis (Trabajo Fin de Grado)** in Mathematics, entitled:

**“Modelización y ajuste de datos reales mediante modelos compartimentales”**

The thesis applies **compartmental models based on systems of ordinary differential equations** to study the evolution of criminal behavior as a socially transmissible phenomenon.

---

## Motivation

Crime is approached as a complex social process influenced by interpersonal interactions and social environments. Inspired by classical epidemiological models, criminal behavior is modeled analogously to infectious diseases, allowing mathematical tools from dynamical systems and epidemiology to be applied to criminological data.

This approach enables both **qualitative analysis** of crime dynamics and **quantitative fitting to real-world data**.

---

## Mathematical Framework

The work is structured around:

- Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs)
- Compartmental population models
- Dynamical systems analysis
- Stability theory
- Parameter estimation via least squares

The thesis begins with a theoretical introduction to **linear ODEs** and the classical **SIR epidemiological model**, which serves as the conceptual foundation.

---

## Compartmental Models

### Classical SIR Model

The SIR model divides the population into:
- **S**: Susceptible individuals
- **I**: Infected individuals
- **R**: Recovered or removed individuals

The model is analyzed both theoretically and through historical epidemic data, including:
- Influenza outbreak in England (1978)
- Plague of Eyam (1665–1666)

These examples illustrate the strengths and limitations of SIR-type models.

---

### Crime Dynamics Model

A novel compartmental model is proposed to describe criminal behavior, dividing the population into:

- **S(t)**: Non-criminal individuals
- **L(t)**: Criminals at liberty
- **P(t)**: Incarcerated individuals

The dynamics are governed by a nonlinear system of ODEs accounting for:
- Social transmission of criminal behavior
- Arrest and incarceration
- Release from prison
- Rehabilitation and desistance
- Demographic inflows and outflows

The model preserves positivity and admits a **Crime-Free Equilibrium (CFE)**.

---

## Basic Reproduction Number (R₀)

A key concept in the analysis is the **basic reproduction number R₀**, interpreted here as the average number of new criminals generated by a single criminal individual.

- Analytical derivation of R₀
- Interpretation from a social dynamics perspective
- Stability analysis of the Crime-Free Equilibrium
- Threshold behavior: crime persistence vs. eradication

---

## Data Fitting and Calibration

The model is calibrated using **real prison population data from Spain (2009–2019)** obtained from official public sources.

Key aspects include:
- Parameter estimation via least squares minimization
- Model validation against observed data
- Error analysis
- Sensitivity analysis of key parameters

The fitted model demonstrates a strong agreement with empirical data and meaningful predictive capabilities.

---

## Predictive Analysis

The predictive performance of the model is evaluated by calibrating parameters using partial datasets and forecasting future crime evolution.

Different training periods are analyzed to assess robustness and stability of predictions.

---

## Conclusions

The results show that compartmental modeling provides a powerful and flexible framework to analyze crime dynamics. The model captures essential mechanisms of criminal propagation and highlights the critical role of social influence, incarceration policies, and rehabilitation rates.

This work illustrates how mathematical modeling can support decision-making in public policy and social intervention strategies.
